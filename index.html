Project Summary (Introduction)

Project Title

Virtual Stress Relief Nature Park

Objective

To create a short, hands-on stress relief experience where users can relax through light, playful interaction instead of passive meditation. The project targets digital fatigue and burnout by offering a calm space with simple tasks that are easy to understand and satisfying to perform.

Target Audience

  • University and college students facing academic pressure
  • Software developers and remote workers with high screen time
  • People who find traditional meditation boring or hard to follow
  • New VR users who prefer simple, gentle interaction instead of complex games

Key Features

  • Apple picking interaction using VR-style hand controllers
  • Physics-based cube stacking that feels natural and calming
  • Nature-inspired environment with soft lighting and ambient sound
  • Pressure-free experience with no scores, timers, or competitive elements
  • Low learning curve suitable for 2–5 minute mental breaks

Technologies Used

  • Unity 3D
  • C# Scripts
  • XR Interaction Toolkit
  • Blender / 3D Assets
  • OBS Studio / Screen Capture
  • GitHub (Version Control)

Process, Challenges & Solutions

Design & Development Process

  • Research: Reviewed existing relaxation and meditation apps to understand gaps in interaction and engagement.
  • Ideation: Selected apple picking and cube stacking as low-pressure, tactile interactions that suit a calm experience.
  • Prototyping: Built an initial Unity scene with basic grabbing mechanics and placeholder assets.
  • Iteration: Improved lighting, physics, environment layout, and interaction timing based on feedback from peers and instructor.
  • Polish: Optimized performance, refined object placement, balanced audio, and prepared the final demo build.

Reflection on Key Challenges

  • Interaction Accuracy: Early versions made grabbing apples inconsistent. We adjusted collider sizes, interaction distances, and XR grab settings until the interaction felt predictable and satisfying.
  • Cube Physics Stability: Stacks collapsed too easily and felt frustrating. We experimented with rigidbody mass, drag, and surface alignment to reach a balance between realism and stability.
  • Performance Drops: Extra objects and dynamic lights reduced frame rate in VR-style testing. We removed unnecessary props, baked lighting where possible, and simplified some meshes to keep the experience smooth.
  • User Comfort: Initial camera and player height caused minor discomfort during movement. We refined the XR rig height, limited sudden rotations, and kept the environment grounded and slow-paced.
  • Incorporating Feedback: Feedback from earlier checkpoints highlighted the need for clearer user flow and improved visibility of interactions. We responded by reworking the layout, adding clearer interaction zones, and enhancing the visual focus around apples and cubes.

Future Improvements

  • Additional scenes such as a rainy forest, snowy field, or riverside area to support different moods.
  • Optional gentle guidance for first-time users (e.g., subtle prompts or a short introductory overlay).
  • Expanded platform support and input testing for different XR devices.

Conclusion

The Virtual Stress Relief Nature Park prototype demonstrates how small, intentional interactions in a calm environment can provide a meaningful mental break for users who are overwhelmed by digital work. By focusing on apple picking and cube stacking, the team created a simple but engaging flow that can be expanded into a full stress-relief product for students, developers, and remote workers.

This project also allowed the team to practice an end-to-end process: from concept and storyboard, through interactive prototyping in Unity, to a polished demo supported by video and documentation. The reflections on challenges and improvements will inform future XR projects that aim to balance usability, comfort, and emotional impact.
